Key Terms

Imagery

Descriptive language appealing to the senses to build vivid pictures.

Simile

Comparison using “like” or “as.” Example: She was as brave as a lion.

Metaphor

Direct comparison without “like” or “as.” Example: Time is a thief.

Theme

The central message or insight of a text.

Symbolism

Use of objects or actions to represent ideas. Example: The green light in The Odyssey.

Allusion

Reference to a well-known person, place, event, literary work, or work of art.

Irony

A contrast between expectation and reality.

Static & Dynamic Characters

Static characters do not change; dynamic characters undergo significant growth.

Conflict

The struggle between opposing forces in a story.

Characterization

Methods authors use to develop characters: direct description and indirect (actions/dialogue).

Foreshadowing

Hints or clues about what will happen later in the story.

Types of Narration

First-person, Third-person Limited, Third-person Omniscient.

Personification

Attribution of human qualities to non-human things. Example: The wind whispered.

Sound Devices

Techniques like alliteration, assonance, consonance.

Enjambment

When a line of poetry continues without a pause to the next line.

End Stop

A pause at the end of a line of poetry, often marked by punctuation.

End Rhyme

Rhyming words at the ends of lines.

Repetition

Repeated words or phrases for emphasis.

Writing Tips

  • Plan with an outline: thesis, main points, evidence, and structure.
  • Craft a clear thesis statement to guide your argument.
  • Use topic sentences at the start of each paragraph.
  • Integrate quotes smoothly: introduce, cite, and explain relevance.
  • Show, don’t tell: use concrete details and sensory language.
  • Vary sentence structures for rhythm and readability.
  • Revise and proofread: check grammar, punctuation, and style.
  • Practice timed writing to prepare for in-class essays.

Examples

Poetry Collection Excerpt

"Beneath the silver moon's pale glow
I wandered roads I used to know."

Shows imagery, end rhyme (glow/know), and enjambment.

Literary Analysis Thesis (Unit 2)

"In The Odyssey, Odysseus’s encounter with the Cyclops demonstrates both his cunning and the limits of mortal agency, highlighting the tension between human will and divine power."

Rhetorical Appeals Outline (Unit 3)

Introduction: Hook + Thesis
Ethos: "As a researcher..."
Pathos: Personal anecdote about risk.
Logos: Statistics on challenge outcomes.
Conclusion: Call to action.

Odyssey Analysis Excerpt (Unit 4)

"When Odysseus blinds Polyphemus, his strategic use of disguise underscores the epic theme of appearance versus reality (Book 9, lines 350–360)."

Short Story Opening (Unit 5)

"Rain tapped against the old mansion’s windows as Clara’s lantern cast tall shadows down the hallway. In her hand, the locket throbbed with memories she could not escape."

Timed Essay Intro (Unit 6)

"In Shakespeare’s Macbeth, ambition drives the tragic hero’s downfall, illustrating how unchecked desire can corrupt the human soul from within."
