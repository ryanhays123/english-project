Writing Tips

Plan First: Before you begin drafting, spend time sketching an outline that identifies your thesis statement, the main points you want to make, and specific evidence you will use to support each point. This roadmap keeps your writing focused, helps you spot gaps in your argument early, and makes the drafting process faster and less frustrating.
Show, Don’t Tell: Instead of stating facts outright (“She was scared”), use vivid, sensory details that allow readers to experience the moment (“Her heart pounded as shadows flickered across the hallway”). Concrete images, active verbs, and sensory language immerse your audience in the scene and make your writing more engaging.
Quote Integration: When you include a quotation from a text, introduce it by naming the speaker or context, present the quote with quotation marks, and then immediately explain how it supports your point. This three-step approach—introduce, quote, explain—ensures your evidence is always tied back to your argument and avoids “orphaned” quotes.
Vary Sentence Structure: Balance short, punchy sentences with longer, more complex ones to create a natural rhythm in your writing. Starting some sentences with transition words, using subordinate clauses, or including rhetorical questions can help maintain reader interest and emphasize key ideas.
Proofread Carefully: After drafting, set your work aside for a short break, then return to check for grammar errors, punctuation mistakes, and consistency with MLA formatting (in-text citations, Works Cited entries, header, and margins). Reading your essay aloud or using a browser’s spell-check tool can help you catch typos you might otherwise miss.

Key Terms

Unit 1: The Power of Words

Plot

The sequence of events in a narrative, typically organized around a conflict and its resolution.

Point of View

The perspective from which a story is told (e.g., first-person, third-person limited, third-person omniscient).

Metaphor

A figure of speech that directly compares two unlike things without using “like” or “as” (e.g., “Time is a thief”).

Indirect Characterization

The process by which a writer shows a character’s personality through actions, dialogue, thoughts, and interactions rather than direct description.

Speaker

In poetry and narrative, the “voice” that addresses the reader, which may or may not be the author.

Unreliable Narrator

A narrator whose credibility is compromised, causing the reader to question the truth of the story being told.

Direct Characterization

The method by which a writer explicitly describes a character’s traits in straightforward terms.

Simile

A comparison between two unlike things using “like” or “as” (e.g., “Brave as a lion”).

Types of Characters

Different roles characters play—static vs. dynamic (whether they change), round vs. flat (depth of personality).

Point of View

Thesis Statement

A clear, focused claim that sets the direction and scope of an academic essay.

Indirect Characterization

(See Unit 1) – pay attention to how character actions reveal motives in analysis essays.

Theme

The central idea, message, or underlying meaning of a literary work.

Quote Integration

(See tips) – essential for supporting claims in literary analysis essays.

Direct Characterization

(See Unit 1)

Imagery

Language that creates sensory experiences for the reader through vivid description.

Narrative Structures

The organizational framework of a story (e.g., linear, flashback, framed narratives).

Unit 3: Risks and Challenges

Ethos

An appeal to the speaker’s credibility or character, showing they are trustworthy.

Body Language

Nonverbal cues—gestures, posture, facial expressions—that reinforce a speaker’s message.

Speech Structure

The organized flow of a speech, typically including an introduction, body, and conclusion with transitions.

Pathos

An appeal to the audience’s emotions, aiming to elicit feelings that support the argument.

Voice

The distinctive style or personality expressed by a writer or speaker.

Logos

An appeal to logic and reason, using data, facts, and rational arguments.

Audience

The intended recipients of a speech or text, whose values and expectations influence tone and content.

Unit 4: The Hero’s Journey

Hero’s Journey

A narrative pattern identified by Joseph Campbell in which a hero goes through stages of departure, initiation, and return.

Invocation of the Muse

A traditional plea to a divine spirit for inspiration, common in epic poetry.

Divine Intervention

Instances where gods or supernatural forces influence human events.

Epic Poem

A long narrative poem celebrating heroic deeds and events of cultural importance.

Epithet

A descriptive phrase expressing a characteristic quality of a person or thing (e.g., “rosy-fingered dawn”).

Symbols

Objects, characters, or actions that represent larger ideas or themes.

Epic Hero

The larger-than-life protagonist of an epic who embodies cultural values.

Epic Similes and Metaphors

Extended comparisons (often spanning lines) used to intensify the heroic stature of the subject.

Repetition

The purposeful reuse of words or phrases for emphasis and rhythm.

Frame Story

A narrative within which one or more other stories are told.

Conflict

The struggle between opposing forces that drives a plot.

Symbolism

Mystery

The element of suspense or uncertainty that keeps readers engaged.

Imagery

Historical Context

Background information about the time period and culture in which a text was written or set.

Mood

The atmosphere or emotional feeling evoked by a text.

Protagonist and Antagonist

The main character of a story (protagonist) and the character or force in opposition (antagonist).

Unit 6: Ambition and Downfall

Tragic Drama

A play in which the protagonist suffers downfall due to a combination of fate, personal flaws, and moral weaknesses.

Hamartia

The protagonist’s tragic error or flaw that leads to their downfall.

Foil Characters

Characters who contrast with the protagonist to highlight particular qualities.

Tragic Hero

A noble or virtuous character who has a fatal flaw and faces a downfall.

Classical, Literary, and Biblical Allusions

References to well-known works or stories that deepen meaning by echoing familiar themes.

Aside

A brief remark by a character to the audience that other characters on stage do not hear.

Tragic Flaw

The character trait—often hubris— that leads the tragic hero to poor decisions.

Stage Directions

Instructions in the script indicating movement, position, or tone of actors on stage.

Soliloquy

A long speech in which a character—usually alone on stage—reveals inner thoughts and feelings.
